Axitinib versus sorafenib as first-line therapy in patients
with metastatic renal-cell carcinoma: a randomised
open-label phase 3 trial
CrossMark

<a id='ff16ae1b-3cb4-4ec7-b6b6-1a9560aba6fe'></a>

Thomas E Hutson, Vladimir Lesovoy, Salman Al-Shukri, Viktor P Stus, Oleg N Lipatov, Angel H Bair, Brad Rosbrook, Connie Chen, Sinil Kim,
Nicholas J Vogelzang

<a id='def151fe-27ae-4c3a-aa6f-12b9e5d2fadc'></a>

Summary
Background In previous clinical trials of patients with metastatic renal-cell carcinoma, patients treated with axitinib as second-line therapy had longer median progression-free survival than those treated with sorafenib. We therefore undertook a phase 3 trial comparing axitinib with sorafenib in patients with treatment-naive metastatic renal-cell carcinoma.

<a id='96f5f389-c1d5-46f5-9046-9229632b789a'></a>

**Methods** In this randomised, open-label, phase 3 trial, patients with treatment-naive, measurable, clear-cell metastatic renal-cell carcinoma from 13 countries were stratified by Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status, and then randomly assigned (2:1) by a centralised registration system to receive axitinib 5 mg twice daily, or sorafenib 400 mg twice daily. The primary endpoint was progression-free survival, assessed by masked independent review committee in the intention-to-treat population. This ongoing trial is registered at ClinicalTrials.gov, NCT00920816.

<a id='33c2e17e-a8c6-43c0-b199-38ac8a8d581e'></a>

Findings Between June 14, 2010, and April 21, 2011, we randomly assigned 192 patients to receive axitinib, and 96 patients to receive sorafenib. The cutoff date for this analysis was July 27, 2012, when 171 (59%) of 288 patients died or had disease progression, as assessed by the independent review committee. There was no significant difference in median progression-free survival between patients treated with axitinib or sorafenib (10.1 months [95% CI 7.2–12.1] vs 6.5 months [4.7–8.3], respectively; stratified hazard ratio 0.77, 95% CI 0.56–1.05). Any-grade adverse events that were more common (≥10% difference) with axitinib than with sorafenib were diarrhoea (94 [50%] of 189 patients vs 38 [40%] of 96 patients), hypertension (92 [49%] vs 28 [29%]), weight decrease (69 [37%] vs 23 [24%]), decreased appetite (54 [29%] vs 18 [19%]), dysphonia (44 [23%] vs ten [10%]), hypothyroidism (39 [21%] vs seven [7%]), and upper abdominal pain (31 [16%] vs six [6%]); those more common with sorafenib than with axitinib included palmar-plantar erythrodysaesthesia (PPE; 37 [39%] of 96 patients vs 50 [26%] of 189), rash (19 [20%] vs 18 [10%]), alopecia (18 [19%] vs eight [4%]), and erythema (18 [19%] vs five [3%]). The most common grade 3 or 4 adverse events in patients treated with axitinib included hypertension (26 [14%] of 189 patients), diarrhoea (17 [9%]), asthenia (16 [8%]), weight decrease (16 [8%]), and PPE (14 [7%]); common grade 3 or 4 adverse events in patients treated with sorafenib included PPE (15 [16%] of 96 patients), diarrhoea (five [5%]), and asthenia (five [5%]). Serious adverse events were reported in 64 (34%) of 189 patients receiving axitinib, and 24 (25%) of 96 patients receiving sorafenib.

<a id='496acc3c-b931-4c0e-9dc1-4eac00cd5e47'></a>

Interpretation Axitinib did not significantly increase progression-free survival in patients with treatment-naive metastatic renal-cell carcinoma compared with those treated with sorafenib, but did demonstrate clinical activity and an acceptable safety profile.

# Introduction
The survival of patients with metastatic clear-cell renal-cell carcinoma has improved with the availability of drugs targeting VEGF, or its receptors (VEGFRs), and mTOR. Currently approved and available mTOR inhibitors include temsirolimus¹ and everolimus;² VEGF/VEGFR-targeted drugs are bevacizumab,³,⁴ sunitinib,⁵ sorafenib,⁶ pazopanib,⁷ and axitinib.⁸ Axitinib (Pfizer Inc, manufactured in Freiburg, Germany), a potent and selective second-generation VEGFR inhibitor,⁹ is approved in the USA,¹⁰ European Union, and elsewhere for treatment of advanced renal-cell carcinoma after failure of one previous systemic therapy. Previously, trials showed median

<a id='cfa5a78e-1e53-4355-8f64-e34ce77cd102'></a>

progression-free survival of 13.7 months with axitinib
in cytokine-refractory metastatic renal-cell carcin-
oma, 11,12 and median progression-free survival of
5.5 months and 5.7 months with sorafenib in cytokine-
refractory and treatment-naive disease, 13 respectively.
On the basis of these data, an ongoing randomised
registrational trial (NCT00920816) of second-line
axitinib versus sorafenib in Asian patients 14 was
amended to add a separate global treatment-naive
population that would be analysed independently from
the second-line population. Sorafenib was chosen as
the comparator because it was available in the regions
where the trial was being done. Here, we report results
from the treatment-naive population.

# Methods
## Study design and patients
This phase 3 randomised, open-label trial evaluated efficacy, safety, and patient-reported outcomes for axitinib versus sorafenib in patients with treatment-naive metastatic renal-cell carcinoma. Patients for this trial were recruited from 13 countries (appendix). Key eligibility criteria included: aged 18 years or older; histologically or cytologically confirmed metastatic renal-cell carcinoma with a clear-cell component; unidimensionally measurable disease by Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST, version 1.0); 15 Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status 0 or 1; at least 2 weeks since previous radiotherapy or surgery; adequate renal, hepatic, and haematological organ function; and life expectancy of at least 12 weeks. Antihypertensive therapy was allowed. Previous nephrectomy was not required. Previous systemic therapy for metastatic renal-cell carcinoma was not allowed; adjuvant interferon or interleukin, or both, were allowed if recurrence occurred more than 6 months after completion. Other exclusion factors included having uncontrolled hypertension (blood pressure higher than 140/90 mm Hg); brain metastases or CNS involvement; a history of other malignancy; or a significant medical history such as myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular accident, or transient ischaemic attack within the past 12 months.

<a id='ca408d38-0ff7-408c-899f-d79e829f84cb'></a>

The trial was undertaken in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, the International Conference on Harmonisation guidelines on Good Clinical Practice, and applicable local regulatory requirements and laws. All patients provided written informed consent. The protocol, amendments, and informed consent forms were approved by an institutional review board or independent ethics committee at each study centre.

<a id='a105928a-13f4-4850-b103-943b54918e8d'></a>

**Randomisation and masking**
Patients were stratified according to ECOG performance
status (0 or 1), and were randomly assigned in a 2:1 ratio by
a centralised registration system to receive either axitinib
5 mg twice daily, or sorafenib 400 mg twice daily.
Randomisation sequences, used by all centres, were
generated by the central randomisation administrator
using permuted blocks with a block size of six (four for
axitinib to two for sorafenib) within each performance
status stratum. Treatment group assignment for an
individual patient was obtained at the time of enrolment
by the investigating site via web interface in a manner that
concealed all past and future assignments. Patients and
investigators were unmasked to treatment. Radiologists
participating in independent review committee assessment
of the primary endpoint were masked to treatment.

<a id='ad1e81c5-d4cd-43cf-91fd-fbc7ec4244e5'></a>

## Procedures
Axitinib was given orally at a starting dose of 5 mg twice daily with food, and sorafenib was given orally at a starting dose of 400 mg twice daily without food, both in

<a id='11528868-f9dd-4536-8328-ad789651d4d5'></a>

4-week cycles. Axitinib doses could be increased first to
7 mg twice daily, and subsequently to 10 mg twice daily,
for patients who had not had any grade two or worse
treatment-related adverse events (as defined by Common
Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events, or CTCAE,
version 3.0) for at least 2 weeks and had blood pressure
150/90 mm Hg or lower. On the basis of adverse events,
or laboratory abnormalities, axitinib dose could be
reduced to 3 mg twice daily, and then 2 mg twice daily,
and sorafenib dose could be reduced to 400 mg once
daily, and then 400 mg every other day. Both drugs were
given according to their approved labelling, such that
dose increases were allowed with axitinib,¹º but not with
sorafenib.¹⁶ Patients who had disease progression but
who had clinical benefit from axitinib or sorafenib were
eligible for continued treatment. Crossover between the
two drugs was not allowed during the study.

<a id='edf4cc72-3012-4d25-8014-06c6bd5c3368'></a>

Baseline tumour assessments included CT or MRI scans of brain, chest, abdomen, and pelvis, and a bone scan. CT or MRI (not including brain) and bone scans (only if baseline lesions were present) were repeated at week 6, week 12, every 8 weeks thereafter, and end of study treatment. All CT, MRI, and bone scans were centrally reviewed by an independent review committee.

<a id='316e436f-3aed-4e7e-a9f0-624e0f831d06'></a>

Physical examinations were done at baseline, day 1 (if day 1 was more than 7 days since baseline), week 2, week 4, every 4 weeks thereafter, and end of study treatment. Blood pressure was monitored at each clinic visit, and twice a day at home by patients. Assessment of ECOG performance status and laboratory tests for haematology, blood chemistry, and urinalysis occurred at baseline, day 1, every 4 weeks, and end of study treatment. Thyroid function tests (free tri-iodothyronine [T3], free thyroxine [T4], and thyroid-stimulating hormone [TSH]) were done at baseline. TSH was then assessed at weeks 2, 4, 8, and 12, and every 8 weeks thereafter. Free T3/T4 was assessed as clinically indicated. Safety was assessed throughout the study, with adverse events graded according to CTCAE.

<a id='5a63228c-dbc1-49b9-a00a-c7a5a4f54212'></a>

The primary endpoint was progression-free survival—
time from randomisation to objective tumour
progression or death—assessed by independent review
committee. Secondary endpoints were assessing the
proportion of patients who achieved an objective
response, response duration, overall survival, safety, and
patient-reported outcomes. Objective response rate
(assessed by independent review committee) was defined
as the proportion of patients with confirmed (persistence
on repeat imaging after at least 4 weeks) complete or
partial response as per RECIST criteria. 15

FKSI-Disease Related Symptoms (FKSI-DRS) subscale,¹⁸
and European Quality of Life-5 Dimensions (EQ-5D)
questionnaires.¹⁹ FKSI-15 consists of 15 items; FKSI-DRS

Statistical analysis
The trial tested the null hypothesis that median progression-free survival with axitinib was 5.5 months versus the alternative hypothesis that it was at least 9.8 months (78% improvement), corresponding to a hazard ratio (HR) of 0.56 (overall one-sided a=0.025).
148 progression-free survival events were needed for a log-rank test with an overall one-sided significance level of 0.025 to have 90% power. An estimated total of 247 patients were to be enrolled.

<a id='bd81853e-3953-49c3-8365-def7e99365b6'></a>

Efficacy and patient-reported outcomes were analysed
in the intention-to-treat (ITT) population (all treatment-
naive patients randomly assigned to groups); safety was
analysed in patients who received at least one dose of
study drug. A third-party independent data monitoring
committee monitored safety on a periodic basis as well
as interim efficacy. At the interim futility analysis,
planned when 74 patients had investigator-assessed
disease progression or death (half of those required for
final analysis), the data monitoring committee could
recommend early trial stopping, or increase the number
of patients required to be observed with disease
progression or death for final analysis by up to 48, based
on conditional power (≥73% and <90%).

<a id='2e034706-fa04-47e0-8528-ec2001cec2d0'></a>

The Kaplan-Meier method was used to estimate
median progression-free survival; two-sided 95% CIs
were calculated. Progression-free survival was compared
with a stratified, one-sided, log-rank test. Cox
proportional hazard model explored the potential
influence of ECOG performance status and patient
characteristics (ie, age, sex, ethnic origin, and Memorial
Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center [MSKCC] risk group) on
progression-free survival. Estimated HR and two-sided
95% CI were provided. A test of the proportional
hazards assumption concluded that there was no
violation of that assumption. Additionally, median
progression-free survival, HRs, and two-sided 95% CIs
were provided for each level of ECOG performance
status and baseline characteristics.

<a id='356f5eb9-494f-400a-861c-f9cbd507fcbd'></a>

The Cochran-Mantel-Haenszel test was used for
stratified analyses comparing the proportion of patients
achieving an objective response between treatments. The
Kaplan-Meier method was used to estimate median
duration of response; two-sided 95% CIs were calculated.
Final analysis of overall survival requires 178 deaths;
these data are currently immature. Patients are being
followed up for survival every 3 months after study
treatment discontinuation.

<a id='f5d93f5c-9589-4766-846b-f16ceeca1f31'></a>

Adverse events and laboratory abnormalities were
reported. Mean (SD) and median FKSI-15, FKSI-DRS,

<a id='622b7cb5-e71e-4143-80e0-b06cbb9334ce'></a>

EQ-5D scores and changes from baseline were reported.
A repeated-measures mixed-effects model compared
patient-reported outcomes between treatments; variables
included treatment, time, and treatment-by-time, with
baseline as covariate. Minimally important differences
were prespecified as five or more points for FKSI-15 and
three or more points for FKSI-DRS, based on published
reports. 17,18 Survival analysis methods evaluated time-to-
deterioration composite endpoints, where time-to-
deterioration was a composite definition consisting of
time to death, tumour progression, or meaningful
worsening in quality of life (defined by minimally
important difference), whichever occurred first. SAS
version 9.2 was used for all statistical analyses.
The study is registered at ClinicalTrials.gov,
NCT00920816.

## Results
Between June 14, 2010, and April 21, 2011, 288 patients with treatment-naive metastatic renal-cell carcinoma were enrolled. The highest enrolment occurred in Ukraine (n=61), Russia (n=58), and India (n=34); with enrolment occurring in ten other countries (appendix). Patient demographic and baseline characteristics were balanced between treatment groups (table 1). 192 patients were randomly assigned to axitinib and 96 to sorafenib (figure 1). Three patients assigned to axitinib did not receive treatment and were not included in the safety population.

The cutoff date for the analyses reported here was July 27, 2012, when 171 (59%) of 288 patients died or had disease progression, as assessed by the independent review committee. The primary endpoint of median progression-free survival, as assessed by independent review committee, was 10.1 months (95% CI 7.2–12.1) with axitinib and 6.5 months (4.7–8.3) with sorafenib (figure 2A). This difference was not significant (stratified HR 0.77, 95% CI 0.56–1.05; one-sided p=0.038). In a prespecified analysis, median progression-free survival was longer with axitinib versus sorafenib in patients with ECOG performance status 0 (figure 2B), but not in patients with ECOG performance status 1 (figure 2C). There were no significant differences in other prespecified analyses (figure 3). In a post-hoc analysis of 250 patients with previous nephrectomy, median progression-free survival as assessed by independent review committee was also improved with axitinib versus sorafenib (figure 2D).

## Discussion
In this randomised phase 3 trial comparing axitinib
versus sorafenib as first-line therapy, although median
progression-free survival was numerically longer with
axitinib than with sorafenib, the difference was not
significant. The trial was designed with the prediction of
an ambitious 4·3-month (78%) improvement in median
progression-free survival over the active comparator
sorafenib. Sorafenib showed longer median progression-
free survival than expected, ⁶,¹³ and axitinib showed shorter
median progression-free survival than previously
observed in patients with cytokine-refractory metastatic
renal-cell carcinoma. ¹¹,¹²

<a id='454a550b-02ba-4d1f-b0f9-f3ba7ca27b6b'></a>

In a prespecified analysis, we observed a 7-1-month
difference in median progression-free survival with
axitinib versus sorafenib in patients with ECOG perfor-
mance status 0, but no difference in patients with per-
formance status 1. To verify that patients with ECOG
performance status 1 were accurately classified, and met
entry criteria, baseline ECOG performance status was
audited at five of the highest enrolling sites; no obvious
evidence of ineligibility based on this measure was noted
from patients' medical records.

A large proportion of patients in AXIS were recruited from North America and western Europe. By contrast, few patients in the current trial were recruited from those areas. Instead, more than half of the patients in this trial were enrolled from eastern Europe, where resource limitations and local practice standards might have affected the type of patient enrolled, or patient management. Patients from lower socioeconomic regions might have had increased disease burdens due to delays in both diagnosis and treatment, thus possibly worsening progression-free survival outcomes, especially in patients with ECOG performance status 1. Another hypothesis might be that eastern European patients who have access to clinical trials might have a higher socioeconomic status, thus leading to increased compliance and better than expected outcomes in patients with ECOG performance status 0. However it is worth noting that ECOG performance status 0 and 1 are broad categories that allow much clinical latitude and remain somewhat subjective. As improved clinical²² and molecular²³ prognostic categories are developed, older classifications (eg, ECOG performance status) are likely to be replaced with more objective ones in future clinical trials.

Although caution should be exercised when comparing across trials, the AXIS⁸ trial appeared to include more patients with MSKCC poor risk than our trial; however, this difference might be explained by use of two different MSKCC risk criteria, based on line of therapy, in the present trial²⁰ versus the AXIS trial.²⁴ More patients in the present trial had axitinib dose increases, and fewer had axitinib dose reductions, compared with patients in the AXIS trial.⁸ This difference might be due to patients receiving first-line versus second-line therapy being able to better tolerate dose increases, and to differences between one of the axitinib dose-escalation criteria (in the AXIS trial, dose increases were not allowed in patients receiving antihypertensive therapy, whereas they were allowed here).

**Interpretation**
To our knowledge, this is the first phase 3 trial evaluating axitinib, a VEGF-receptor
inhibitor, in patients with treatment-naive metastatic renal-cell carcinoma. Axitinib was
found to have clinical activity, and a manageable safety profile in these patients. Median
progression-free survival with axitinib in the overall population is similar to that reported
in other first-line studies evaluating sunitinib⁵ or pazopanib⁷ for the treatment of
metastatic renal-cell carcinoma. Given its clinical activity and differentiated toxicity profile,
axitinib is a welcome addition to the treatment armamentarium for patients with
metastatic renal-cell carcinoma.

Baseline mean FKSI-15 and FKSI-DRS scores in the present trial were similar to those observed in a general US population,26 and so improvement in quality of life was not expected. Maintenance of quality of life was regarded as a good outcome. A consistent finding in observed mean FKSI-15 and FKSI-DRS scores was shown here as in the AXIS trial,27 in which quality of life worsened at the end of treatment with both axitinib and sorafenib, presumably because disease progression worsened quality of life. In the longitudinal analysis, postbaseline scores for patient-reported outcomes were similar between axitinib and sorafenib over time, consistent with results from AXIS.27 Unlike the AXIS trial,8 although the time-to-deterioration composite endpoints of time to death, tumour progression, or meaningful worsening in quality of life appeared longer for axitinib versus sorafenib, the differences were not significant; thus for both patient groups clinical efficacy (progression-free survival) was achieved without sacrificing quality of life.
