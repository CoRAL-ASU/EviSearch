Axitinib Versus Sorafenib in First-Line Metastatic
Renal Cell Carcinoma: Overall Survival From a

# Abstract

In a randomized phase III trial, axitinib did not significantly improve progression-free survival over sorafenib in treatment-naive patients with metastatic renal cell carcinoma. This updated analysis confirmed clinical activity of axitinib with an acceptable safety profile after long-term treatment. Although axitinib did not prolong survival over sorafenib, axitinib might provide an acceptable first-line treatment option in patients with good performance status.

<a id='dff9965e-1158-4e7d-a72b-6673303796b5'></a>

**Background:** In a randomized phase III trial in treatment-naive patients with metastatic renal cell carcinoma (RCC), axitinib versus sorafenib yielded numerically longer progression-free survival (median, 10.1 vs. 6.5 months; hazard ratio [HR], 0.77; 1-sided P = .038) and significantly higher objective response rate (32% vs. 15%; 1-sided P = .0006). In this article, we report overall survival (OS) and updated safety results. **Patients and Methods:** Previously untreated patients with metastatic RCC (n = 288), stratified according to Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status (ECOG PS; 0 vs. 1), were randomized 2:1 to receive axitinib 5 mg twice per day (b.i.d.; n = 192) or sorafenib 400 mg b.i.d. (n = 96). **Results:** Median OS (95% confidence interval [CI]) was 21.7 months (18.0-31.7) with axitinib versus 23.3 months (18.1-33.2) with sorafenib (stratified HR, 0.995; 95% CI, 0.731-1.356; 1-sided P = .4883). Among patients with ECOG PS of 0, median OS was numerically longer with axitinib than with sorafenib (41.2 vs. 31.9 months; HR, 0.811, 1-sided P = .1748), whereas among patients with ECOG PS 1, median OS was shorter with axitinib than with sorafenib (14.2 vs. 19.8 months; HR, 1.203; 1-sided; P = .7973). Incidence and severity of common adverse events were consistent with previous reports. **Conclusion:** OS was similar between axitinib and sorafenib in treatment-naive patients with metastatic RCC, and no new safety signals emerged.

# Patients and Methods
The details of this randomized, open-label phase III study (ClinicalTrials.gov, NCT00920816), conducted in compliance with the Declaration of Helsinki, the International Conference on Harmonisation guidelines on Good Clinical Practice, and other requirements, have been reported previously.² Briefly, treatment-naive patients with confirmed clear-cell mRCC, measurable disease per Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors version 1.0, ECOG PS 0 or 1, and blood pressure ≤ 140/90 mm Hg were eligible for the study. Patients were stratified according to ECOG PS and randomly assigned (2:1), using a centralized registration system, to receive twice-daily axitinib 5 mg or sorafenib 400 mg orally in 4-week cycles. The axitinib dose could be up- or down-titrated under predefined clinical conditions, whereas the sorafenib dose could only be reduced.
The primary end point of this study, PFS assessed by a blinded independent review committee, has been reported²; OS was a secondary end point.

<a id='baf9c70e-aba8-4cb7-9532-e9ee1198899f'></a>

Radiologic tumor assessments were performed at screening,
weeks 6 and 12, and every 8 weeks thereafter. Safety was monitored
throughout the study, and AEs were graded per Common Termi-
nology Criteria for Adverse Events version 3.0. Patients were fol-
lowed for survival every 3 months after discontinuing study
treatment until at least 3 years after randomization of the last
patient.

<a id='d104821b-32e3-45f6-9f56-83dec8d88dc7'></a>

The estimated sample size of approximately 247 patients,
which was required for this study to have 90% power to detect a
difference in PFS (overall 1-sided α = 0.025), was also sufficient
to have 80% power to detect a difference in OS after 178 deaths,
assuming an approximately 59% improvement in OS with axiti-
nib versus sorafenib (median OS, 27 vs. 17 months), a follow-up
period of approximately 37 months, and no dropout. OS in each
treatment arm was analyzed with the Kaplan—Meier method and
compared with a 1-sided log-rank test, stratified according to
ECOG PS.

## Results
A total of 288 treatment-naive patients with mRCC were randomly assigned to the axitinib (n = 192) or sorafenib (n = 96) arm.² Three patients in the axitinib arm did not receive treatment and were excluded from the safety analysis. At the time of the data cutoff date for OS analysis (December 18, 2014), 161 (84%) patients in the axitinib arm versus 83 (86%) in the sor-afenib arm discontinued study treatment, mostly because of

<a id='c8edda39-32ec-4ab9-b5a3-4e43bf03ba4f'></a>

objective progression or disease relapse (50% vs. 63%), followed
by deaths (11% vs. 8%), refusal of treatment for reason other
than AE (8% vs. 3%), and AEs (6% vs. 4%; Figure 1). After
discontinuation of study treatment, comparable proportions of
patients in each treatment arm received follow-up systemic
treatments (Table 1).

<a id='87240ab2-86fc-48af-b3f8-303fb27c5a9e'></a>

In all, 115 (60%) and 62 (65%) patients in the axitinib and
sorafenib arm, respectively, died mainly from disease progression,
of whom 38 (20%) and 10 (10%) patients, respectively, died
while receiving treatment. Median OS (95% CI) was 21.7 months
(18.0-31.7) with axitinib compared with 23.3 months (18.1-
33.2) with sorafenib (stratified HR, 0.995; 95% CI, 0.731-1.356;
1-sided P .4883; Figure 2A). In the ECOG PS 0 subgroup,
axitinib showed improved survival compared with sorafenib, but
this was not observed in the ECOG PS 1 subgroup (Figure 2B
and C).

<a id='8eadccde-8ecd-4bda-b0c7-df9180b90f96'></a>

Common (≥ 30% of patients) treatment-emergent, all-causality AEs were diarrhea, hypertension, weight decrease, and fatigue with axitinib, and diarrhea, palmar-plantar erythrodysesthesia, and hypertension with sorafenib (Table 2). Among the common all-grade AEs, AEs reported more frequently (≥ 10%) with axitinib than with sorafenib were diarrhea, hypertension, weight decrease, decreased appetite, dysphonia, and hypothyroidism; those more frequently reported with sorafenib than with axitinib were palmar-plantar erythrodysesthesia and rash. Grade ≥ 3 hypertension was more common with axitinib than with sorafenib, as was weight decrease and fatigue, whereas Grade ≥ 3 palmar-plantar erythrodysesthesia was reported more frequently with sorafenib. Diarrhea was reported as the most common all-causality serious AE in 7 (4%) axitinib-treated patients and in 2 (2%) sorafenib-treated patients.

# Discussion
This randomized phase III trial showed that OS was similar between axitinib and sorafenib in the first-line treatment of mRCC. In the predefined subgroup analysis, a 9.3-month difference in median OS with axitinib over sorafenib was observed in patients with ECOG PS 0, but the difference was in favor of sorafenib in patients with ECOG PS 1. A similar trend favoring axitinib over sorafenib in patients with ECOG PS 0, but no difference in patients with ECOG PS 1, was reported for PFS, the primary end point of the study, in the primary analysis. Within each treatment arm, median OS was longer in patients with ECOG PS 0 versus ECOG PS 1 (axitinib, 41.2 vs. 14.2 months; sorafenib, 31.9 vs. 19.8 months). Although ECOG PS is one of the prognostic factors identified for PFS and OS in patients with RCC treated with VEGF pathway-targeted tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs)³, it is somewhat subjective in nature. It is unclear if the results of the ECOG PS subgroup analyses in our study indicate a preferential effect of axitinib over sorafenib in patients with more favorable prognosis, and further investigation is warranted.

It is unclear why median OS for axitinib and sorafenib was shorter in this study than in other studies, but reasons would likely be multifactorial. In addition, patients and investigators were not blinded to treatment, which could have biased safety assessments.

### Clinical Practice Points

*   Axitinib, a potent and selective inhibitor of VEGF receptors 1-3,
    is approved by the FDA for treatment of patients with previously
    treated mRCC.
*   In a randomized phase III trial, which evaluated the efficacy and
    safety of axitinib compared with sorafenib in treatment-naive
    patients with mRCC, clinical activity of axitinib was shown,
    although the difference in the primary end point of PFS did not
    reach the predefined significance.
*   In the current updated analysis, axitinib did not prolong survival
    compared with sorafenib, but clinical activity of axitinib in pa-
    tients with previously untreated mRCC was confirmed and its
    safety after long-term treatment was shown.
*   The overall results of this randomized phase III trial provides
    supporting evidence for further clinical development of axitinib
    in first-line treatment of mRCC.
*   The landscape for treatment of patients with mRCC is changing
    rapidly with several available targeted therapies, including axiti-
    nib. Long-term safety and efficacy data might be useful in
    guiding treatment decisions for patients with advanced RCC.
