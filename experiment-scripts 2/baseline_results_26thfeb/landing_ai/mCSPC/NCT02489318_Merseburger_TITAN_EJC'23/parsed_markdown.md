Abstract Background: Whether disease burden in patients with metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer (mCSPC) predicts treatment outcomes is unknown. We assessed apalutamide treatment effect in TITAN patients with mCSPC by disease volume, metastasis number and timing of metastasis presentation.

<a id='4255ed0d-e1f4-4c0d-8728-d40be120468e'></a>

Methods: These protocol-defined and post hoc analyses of the phase III randomised TITAN study evaluated clinical outcomes in patients receiving 240 mg/day apalutamide (n = 525) or placebo (n = 527) plus androgen-deprivation therapy (ADT). Subgroups were defined by volume (high: visceral and ≥1 bone metastases or ≥4 bone lesions with ≥1 beyond vertebral column/pelvis), development of metastases per conventional imaging (synchronous: at initial diagnosis; metachronous: after localised disease) and oligometastases (≤5 bone-only metastases) or polymetastases (> 5 in bone ± other locations or ≤5 in bone plus other locations). Overall survival (OS), radiographic or second progression-free survival, and time to prostate-specific antigen progression or castration resistance were assessed using Cox proportional hazards models.

<a id='b6dfcbcf-45a3-4a56-ae05-984138dbb1fb'></a>

Results: Of 1052 patients, 63%, 81%, 54%, 27%, 5.7%, and 8.0% had high-volume, synchronous, synchronous/high-volume, synchronous/low-volume, metachronous/high-volume, and metachronous/low-volume disease, respectively. The OS benefit favoured apalutamide plus ADT versus ADT alone in synchronous/high-volume (HR = 0.68 [95% confidence interval: 0.53-0.87]; p = 0.002), synchronous/low-volume (0.65 [0.40-1.05]; p = 0.08), metachronous/high-volume (0.69 [0.33-1.44]; p = 0.32) and metachronous/low-volume (0.22 [0.09-0.55]; p = 0.001) subgroups. Apalutamide improved other clinical outcomes regardless of subgroup, with similar safety profiles. Most favourable outcomes were observed in oligometastatic disease.

<a id='c30a3054-c7c4-48f7-9b2b-fe486d312e02'></a>

**Conclusion:** TITAN patients derived a robust benefit with apalutamide plus ADT regardless of disease volume and timing of metastasis presentation without differences in safety, sup-porting early apalutamide intensification in mCSPC.

1. Introduction
Metastatic burden is prognostic for for patients with metastatic castration-sensitive prostate cancer (mCSPC). High-volume presentation with metastases at initial diagnosis (synchronous disease) and low-volume presentation with metastases occurring after primary treatment for localised prostate cancer (metachronous disease) have been associated with less and more favourable prognosis, respectively [1]. Oligometastatic versus polymetastatic disease and specific anatomical locations of metastases may also be important prognostic variables, although further validation of their prognostic value is needed [1–3].

<a id='e115cf52-1b86-43c7-8e99-503010046e55'></a>

Effective approaches for patients with mCSPC and var-
ious disease characteristics are being investigated. Thus,
treatment intensification with abiraterone acetate plus pre-
dnisone (AAP) added to standard of care (androgen-depri-
vation therapy [ADT] ± docetaxel), with or without
radiation therapy, in PEACE-1 improved radiographic
progression-free survival (rPFS) in both high- and low-vo-
lume subgroups and overall survival (OS) in the high-volume
subgroup [4]. The Advanced Prostate Cancer Consensus
Conference concurred on the importance of distinguishing
synchronous from metachronous low-volume mCSPC and
strongly agreed on treating both groups with ADT plus an

<a id='464e1c96-fd20-46a6-9500-cf4e7b9e3085'></a>

androgen receptor (AR) pathway inhibitor and adding pri-
mary radiation therapy for synchronous/low-volume
mCSPC [5]. Long follow-up analysis of PEACE-1 showed
that the addition of prostate radiotherapy to systemic
therapy with AAP plus ADT, with or without docetaxel, for
synchronous/low-volume mCSPC did not improve OS but
improved rPFS, time to castration resistance and rates of
serious genitourinary events [6]. Treatment intensification
with enzalutamide and ADT in ARCHES improved out-
comes in mCSPC irrespective of volume, risk, metastatic
burden or metachronous/synchronous disease presentation
[7-13]. Addition of docetaxel to ADT increased OS in high-
volume but not low-volume mCSPC in CHAARTED and
GETUG-AFU15 studies [14-17]. Meta-analysis of these two
studies and STAMPEDE showed that doublet treatment
improved progression-free survival and OS in all patients,
except the metachronous/low-volume subgroup, but was
best for patients with high-volume disease [18,19]. Triplet
treatment with darolutamide, ADT, and docetaxel in
ARASENS improved OS in high-volume and synchronous/
high-volume disease [20]. Enzalutamide plus ADT showed
survival benefits in patients with metachronous/low-volume
disease compared with nonsteroidal antiandrogens plus
ADT in ENZAMET [3]. Enzalutamide plus ADT combi-
nation was also more beneficial for ENZAMET patients

## 2. Material and methods

The full methodology of TITAN, including detailed inclusion criteria, has been published [22,23]. Briefly, patients with mCSPC, ≥1 distant metastases on conventional imaging and Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status 0 or 1 were included. TITAN was conducted at 260 sites in 23 countries in accordance with current International Council for Harmonisation guidelines for Good Clinical Practice and according to the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. Institutional review boards approved the study, and patients provided written informed consent. Dual primary endpoints were rPFS and OS; exploratory end-points included time to PSA progression and PFS2. Additional details can be found in the Supplementary Methods.

<a id='229e02a5-2e8d-4d8a-8519-c6399ff89a56'></a>

These protocol-defined and *post hoc* analyses assessed efficacy in the intent-to-treat (ITT) populations using rPFS (time to radiographic progressive disease or death from any cause), OS (time from randomisation to the date of death from any cause), PFS2 (time from randomisation to first occurrence of disease progression on first subsequent therapy for prostate cancer or death), confirmed PSA decline (decline of ≥50% or ≥90% from baseline or decline to ≤0.2 ng/mL achieved at any time during the study and confirmed ≥4 weeks later), time to PSA progression (time from date of randomisation to date of PSA progression based on Prostate Cancer Working Group 2 (PCWG2) criteria [27]) and time to castration resistance (time from date of randomisation to date of PSA progression, based on PCWG2 criteria [27]). Imaging was assessed per Response Evaluation Criteria In Solid Tumors, version 1.1, modified by PCWG2 criteria [27]. Deep PSA decline was defined as either ≥90% PSA decline or PSA ≤0.2 ng/mL.

<a id='662eea43-e15a-4329-a283-e0b0a7dc8e82'></a>

Treatment effect of apalutamide was analysed by
disease volume, synchronous/metachronous metastases
or both. High-volume disease was defined as either
visceral metastases with ≥1 bone lesion or ≥4 bone le-
sions including one outside of the vertebral column or
pelvis (modified CHAARTED criteria [16]). Patients
who did not meet the high-volume disease criteria were
considered to have low-volume disease. Synchronous
disease was defined as metastases at initial diagnosis;
metachronous disease was defined as metastases devel-
oped after localised disease. The effect of the number of
metastatic lesions was assessed in apalutamide-treated
patients with oligometastases (≤5 in bone only) versus
those with polymetastases (>5 in bone with/without
other locations or ≤5 in bone and other locations). The
subgroup analyses by volume (high or low) and timing
of metastasis presentation (synchronous or metachro-
nous) were defined in the TITAN protocol; the other
subgroup analyses were post hoc.

<a id='26d97214-b423-4a21-8ea3-40caa79be117'></a>

Treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs), defined
as AEs occurring on or after the first dose of the study
drug through 30 days after the last study treatment,
were graded according to National Cancer Institute
Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events
version 4.0.3 to grade 1 or mild (symptoms that were
easily tolerated, causing minimal discomfort and not
interfering with everyday activities), grade 2 or mod-
erate (sufficient discomfort was present to cause inter-
ference with normal activity), grade 3 or severe (extreme
distress, causing significant impairment of functioning
or incapacitation; prevents normal everyday activities),
grade 4 or life-threatening (urgent intervention in-
dicated) and grade 5 or death.

<a id='9d6e6879-9557-47c8-9338-7320547c30b2'></a>

A TEAE is considered a serious adverse event (SAE)
based on International Council for Harmonisation and
European Union Guidelines on Pharmacovigilance for
Medicinal Products for Human Use if, at any dose, it
results in death or in persistent or significant disability/
incapacity, requires inpatient hospitalisation or pro-
longation of existing hospitalisation, or is life-threa-
tening, a congenital anomaly/birth defect, a suspected
transmission of any infectious agent via a medicinal
product or medically important. A medically important
event may be considered an SAE if it jeopardises the
patient or may require intervention to prevent the above
outcomes despite not necessarily being immediately life-
threatening or resulting in death or hospitalisation.

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Demographics and disease characteristics at baseline

A total of 1052 patients were randomised in a 1:1 ratio to receive either 240 mg/day apalutamide (_n_ = 525) or placebo (_n_ = 527), plus ongoing ADT, between 15th December 2015 and 25th July 2017 (Fig. S1). The pre-specified first interim analysis (final analysis for rPFS) occurred at the cutoff on 23rd November 2018, after the number of required events occurred within 22.7 mo of follow-up. The prespecified final analysis occurred at the cutoff on 7th September 2020 after the number of required deaths had occurred within 44.0 mo of follow-up.

<a id='8c6456db-a40b-4ca7-aa27-9eb322976c65'></a>

The majority of TITAN patients had high-volume
(63%; 660/1052) and synchronous (81%; 852/1052) disease.
Four subgroups stratified by disease volume and timing of
metastasis presentation comprised 54% (567/1052; syn-
chronous/high-volume), 27% (285/1052; synchronous/low-
volume disease), 5.7% (60/1052; metachronous/high-
volume), and 8.0% (84/1052; metachronous/low-volume)
of patients. Major disease characteristics pertinent to this
analysis were balanced across treatment groups and sub-
groups (Table 1). Bone-only metastases were present in
558 (53%) patients, including 286 (27%) with ≤5 and 272
(26%) with > 5 lesions. Metastases in bone plus visceral
locations were present in 128 (12%) patients. Only 11%
(58) of apalutamide-treated patients had up to six cycles of
docetaxel prior to apalutamide plus ADT; of those, 44
patients had high-volume disease and 40 had high-volume/
synchronous disease. Of patients with high-volume dis-
ease, 81% (532/660) had > 5 bone lesions, and of patients
with low-volume disease, 91% (356/392) had ≤5 bone
lesions.

Apalutamide-treated patients from the metachronous sub- group or from the low-volume subgroup had improved OS compared with those from synchronous or high-volume subgroups, respectively: HR =0.62 [95%CI: 0.39-0.997], p = 0.046 and 0.36 [0.25-0.51], p < 0.001. This benefit was seen for the other outcomes (data not shown). The treat- ment effect (apalutamide plus ADT versus ADT alone) for OS and rPFS was homogeneous across high- and low-vo- lume (interaction effect p-values 0.262 and 0.194 for OS and rPFS, respectively) or across metachronous synchronous subgroups (interaction effect p-values 0.075 and 0.526, re- spectively).

3.3. Effect of the number and location of metastases on
outcomes in apalutamide-treated patients

<a id='c7afb7e8-65a4-410d-88a3-ce24780be54b'></a>

Apalutamide-treated patients with oligometastatic disease
with ≤5 bone-only metastases had the most favourable OS
and other outcomes versus those with polymetastatic dis-
ease with either > 5 bone-only metastases, > 5 bone me-
tastases plus metastases in other organs or ≤5 bone
metastases plus additional metastases in other organs
(Fig. 2). Poorest outcomes were observed in patients with
polymetastatic disease with > 5 bone metastases plus me-
tastases in other locations. Compared with patients with
bone-only disease (n = 289), those with metastases in bone
plus visceral plus other locations (n = 56) or those with bone
plus metastases in other locations (n = 180) had shorter OS
(HR = 2.63 [95% CI: 1.68–4.10] and 1.90 [1.37–2.63]) and
rPFS (3.42 [2.10–5.55] and 2.22 [1.52–3.25]; p < 0.001 for
all). Similar results were observed for other outcomes (data
not shown).

<a id='c94cb1e4-d455-4a69-9cfe-0448dc68cce1'></a>

In placebo-treated patients, the outcomes generally
followed the pattern in apalutamide-treated patients,
except for patients with ≤5 bone metastases plus other
locations, in whom results were inconclusive (Fig. S8).

4. Discussion

Apalutamide added to concurrent ADT resulted in clinical benefits in the overall TITAN population [22,23]. These protocol-defined and _post hoc_ analyses of TITAN patients with mCSPC confirm apalutamide benefit across a range of clinical prognostic subgroups with varying disease characteristics based on disease volume, timing of metastatic presentation at diagnosis and number of metastases. Deep PSA decline and consistent efficacy across all outcomes were also observed with apalutamide irrespective of disease volume or timing of metastasis occurrence. Although patients with low-volume or metachronous disease had the greatest benefit with apalutamide plus ADT, patients from other subgroups, including those with synchronous/high-volume disease, a subgroup that has poor survival prognosis with ADT alone [1], also derived benefit from apalutamide plus ADT. Our findings are consistent with deep PSA decline observed in the majority of the TITAN population which translated into improved long-term outcomes [28–30]. The safety profile of apalutamide across subgroups remained consistent with previous reports [22,23]. These data support the efficacy and safety of treatment intensification with apalutamide in the diverse population of patients with mCSPC.

<a id='3f61db45-3d7f-4a94-8950-784b252a0d55'></a>

We observed clinical benefit in terms of estimated HR favouring apalutamide across all subgroups, although nominal p-values for OS, rPFS and PFS2 in the metachronous/high-volume subgroup or for OS in the synchronous/low-volume subgroup were > 0.05. The small number of patients and events in these subgroups decreased the statistical power, and therefore the conclusions for these subgroups should be confirmed in studies with larger numbers of patients and longer follow-ups. However, HR values for OS in metachronous/high-volume and synchronous/low-volume subgroups were very similar to those in the synchronous/high-volume subgroup, supporting the consistency of benefit across three subgroups. The lack of interaction with high-/low-volume or synchronous/metachronous disease presentation further supports the consistent treatment effect of apalutamide.

<a id='46a521f5-e39a-4671-b22b-5fd7d5325c97'></a>

Similar subgroup analyses from ARCHES assessed the efficacy of enzalutamide and ADT in mCSPC [10,11,31]. Those analyses also showed that, compared with ADT alone, enzalutamide added to ADT improved rPFS and other secondary end-points regardless of volume, oligometastatic/polymetastatic disease, metachronous/synchronous metastases or pattern of metastatic spread.

<a id='428955e1-e5ad-42f4-bf3a-6d3688551c2a'></a>

Treatment regimens suggested for patients with
mCSPC depend on disease burden, and associated risks
are still being investigated. Among the four subgroups

<a id='ea3e6e6d-d8c1-429a-b313-f0af91c337b6'></a>

stratified by timing of metastasis presentation and disease
volume, patients with metachronous/low-volume mCSPC
had the lowest and those with synchronous/high-volume
disease had the highest risk of death or developing cas-
tration resistance [1]. The metachronous/low-volume sub-
group did not benefit from doublet treatment with
docetaxel and ADT compared with ADT alone [19] but
did from the enzalutamide with ADT combination com-
pared with nonsteroidal antiandrogen and ADT. Of note,
the latter benefit was observed in ENZAMET patients
[3,21], approximately half of whom received concurrent
docetaxel as part of their standard of care [3,21,32]. We
observed a treatment effect of the apalutamide and ADT
combination compared with ADT alone with nominal
two-sided p < 0.05 across all low-volume subgroups, in-
cluding those with metachronous or synchronous char-
acteristics.

<a id='9d28cb0e-010c-4dbe-8b79-4901b1321915'></a>

Doublet or triplet combination treatments with
docetaxel and ADT or an AR pathway synthesis in-
hibitor appear to be most effective in poor-prognosis
high-volume subgroups. The meta-analysis of docetaxel
and ADT studies showed that the docetaxel effect is
greater with higher volume and bulkiness of disease, as
defined by increasing T-stage, and, to a lesser degree,
with synchronous metastases [19]. Triplet treatment
with docetaxel, ADT and AAP or docetaxel, ADT and
darolutamide is now recommended for patients with
mCSPC who are fit for docetaxel based on the PEACE-
1 and ARASENS studies, respectively [4,33,34]. How-
ever, patient populations that would benefit from triplet
therapy still need to be defined. The updated subgroup
analysis of ARASENS did not show significantly im-
proved OS in metachronous/high-volume disease with
triplet treatment [20]. We observed a treatment effect of
apalutamide based on hazard ratio and p-value in the
synchronous/high-volume subgroup across all assessed
end-points. The efficacy of triplet apalutamide, doc-
etaxel and ADT treatment is the subject of future stu-
dies; however, we have shown that OS and other clinical
outcomes in the overall and case-matched TITAN po-
pulations and in the high-volume subpopulation were
similar regardless of prior docetaxel [35]. As the syn-
chronous/high-volume subgroup represents 91% (40/44)
of the high-volume population, similar results by prior
docetaxel in this subgroup would likely be observed.
Whether concurrent docetaxel provides additional ben-
efit to apalutamide plus ADT must be assessed pro-
spectively.

<a id='67f4236e-5347-4aaa-b9bf-b7466b8e105b'></a>

The limitations of this analysis include the _post hoc_
nature of some of these analyses, the relatively small size
of some subgroups and the relatively short follow-up of
the better-prognosis synchronous/low-volume sub-
group, which limited interpretability. Metastatic burden
was defined by conventional imaging only; use of more
sensitive measures may have affected the results.
Additional prospective analyses of large patient popu-
lations are needed to confirm our findings.

## 5. Conclusions

This protocol-defined and _post hoc_ analysis of TITAN patients with mCSPC showed that apalutamide plus ADT produced a robust long-term benefit regardless of stratification by disease volume, synchronous/meta-chronous metastases, or combination of both factors. Among patients treated with apalutamide, those with low-volume disease or metachronous metastases derived greater benefits than those with high-volume disease or synchronous metastases according to the hazard ratio. However, in patients with synchronous/high-volume disease, the addition of apalutamide to ADT also resulted in significantly improved survival and other clinical outcomes. Our findings are consistent with a robust benefit/risk profile of apalutamide in mCSPC, supporting effective and safe early treatment in all subgroups of patients with mCSPC.
